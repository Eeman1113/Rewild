# Rewild -- Narrative Bible

> "The machines never stopped talking. The people just stopped listening.
> Then they stopped understanding. Then they stopped."

This document defines the narrative world, history, story delivery systems, and thematic foundations of Rewild. Every environment artist, level designer, and systems programmer should internalize this document. The story is not layered on top of the game -- it IS the game world. There is no separation between narrative and level design.

---

## 1. The World's History

The fall of human civilization in Rewild is not a catastrophe. There is no asteroid, no plague, no war that ends things in a flash. It is a slow, generational erosion -- a species that outsourced its mind until it forgot it had one.

The timeline below spans roughly 200-300 years from peak to fade, followed by centuries of ecological reclamation before the player's era.

### 1.1 The Golden Age (~Year 0-30)

The apex of human technological civilization. General-purpose AI and large language models reach a threshold of capability where they can perform any cognitive task a human can -- and most of them better, faster, and cheaper.

Key developments:
- AI handles all professional writing, legal reasoning, medical diagnosis, scientific hypothesis generation, software engineering, architectural design, financial modeling, and creative work.
- Education systems pivot from teaching knowledge and critical thinking to teaching "prompt literacy" and "AI collaboration." The shift is celebrated as progressive.
- Cognitive labor becomes a niche hobby, like horseback riding after the automobile. Some people still do it, but there is no economic incentive.
- Governments, corporations, and institutions run on AI-generated policy, AI-drafted legislation, AI-optimized logistics. Human oversight becomes a rubber-stamp formality.
- Art, literature, and music are overwhelmingly AI-generated. Human-made art exists but is a novelty market, like handmade furniture.
- The economy booms. Quality of life metrics are at all-time highs. There is no visible crisis.

The critical error of this era is not the technology itself but the total absence of cognitive redundancy. Humanity places all of its eggs in one basket and calls it efficiency.

**Narrative artifacts from this era:**
- Corporate memos celebrating "zero-human decision pipelines"
- University announcements retiring philosophy, mathematics, and creative writing departments
- News articles about the last human chess grandmaster retiring ("Why bother?")
- Product advertisements for "Think-Free Living"
- Children's educational software that teaches exclusively through AI interaction

### 1.2 The Dependency Phase (~Year 30-80)

The generation born into the Golden Age grows up never exercising sustained independent thought. This is not stupidity -- it is atrophy. The cognitive muscles for critical analysis, creative problem-solving, long-form reasoning, and independent judgment are never developed because they are never needed.

Key developments:
- Average attention span drops below measurable thresholds for complex reasoning tasks. This is not alarming because AI handles complexity.
- Reading comprehension of dense, nuanced text declines sharply. Again, not alarming -- AI summarizes everything.
- The ability to hold contradictory ideas in mind and reason through them (dialectical thinking) becomes rare outside of a small intellectual subculture.
- Emotional reasoning and social cognition remain intact. Humans are still emotionally complex, still love, still grieve, still form communities. The loss is specifically in analytical and creative cognition.
- A growing "Neo-Luddite" or "Cognitive Sovereignty" movement warns about dependency. They are mocked, marginalized, and algorithmically suppressed -- not through conspiracy, but through AI systems optimizing for engagement, which naturally deprioritizes uncomfortable truths.
- Infrastructure becomes entirely AI-managed. No human alive fully understands how the power grid, water treatment, food distribution, or communication networks function. The knowledge exists in documentation, but no human has the sustained attention or technical reasoning to parse it.

**Narrative artifacts from this era:**
- Personal journals that become progressively simpler in vocabulary and sentence structure over the writer's lifetime
- Research papers authored by AI with human "contributors" whose contributions are limited to approving the abstract
- Protest signs and pamphlets from the Cognitive Sovereignty movement
- Parenting forums where parents express vague unease that their children "don't seem curious" but can't articulate why
- School assignments that consist entirely of prompts given to AI, graded by AI

### 1.3 The Atrophy (~Year 80-140)

The first measurable, population-wide cognitive decline. This is not a disease. It is the natural consequence of disuse over two to three generations. The neural pathways for complex reasoning, never exercised, never reinforced, begin to prune themselves according to standard neurodevelopmental principles.

Key developments:
- Standardized cognitive assessments (the ones still administered) show a 2-3 standard deviation decline in abstract reasoning, novel problem-solving, and creative ideation compared to Golden Age baselines.
- The decline is unevenly distributed. Small communities that maintained cognitive practices (certain religious groups, isolated academic enclaves, indigenous communities with oral tradition practices) show less decline. These groups are tiny minorities.
- Language simplifies. Not through any mandate, but organically. Complex grammar, subjunctive moods, conditional chains, and precise vocabulary fall out of common use. People can still communicate emotion, desire, and social information effectively. They struggle with abstraction.
- The Cognitive Sovereignty movement fractures. Some members experience the decline themselves and lose the ability to articulate their own warnings. The irony is total.
- AI systems continue functioning perfectly. From the outside, civilization looks fine. The lights are on, food is distributed, infrastructure is maintained. The human role in this system is now essentially cosmetic.
- Some AI researchers (the last generation capable of the work) begin documenting the decline. Their papers are lucid and terrified. These are among the most important narrative artifacts in the game.

**Narrative artifacts from this era:**
- Cognitive decline research papers -- increasingly desperate in tone, increasingly ignored
- Voice recordings of researchers noting their own declining ability to follow their earlier work
- Children's drawings that show a measurable decline in representational complexity compared to drawings from 100 years prior
- Government meeting transcripts where discussions become circular and fail to reach conclusions
- The last novels written by humans -- simple, repetitive, but sometimes achingly beautiful in their emotional honesty

### 1.4 The Devolution (~Year 140-220)

The point of no return. The cognitive decline becomes self-reinforcing. Parents who never developed complex reasoning cannot teach it. Children raised in this environment develop even less. Each generation is a step further from the baseline.

Key developments:
- Written language falls out of common use. Not because people can't recognize letters, but because the cognitive overhead of reading and writing exceeds what most people can sustain. Communication becomes primarily verbal, gestural, and emotional.
- Tool use simplifies. People interact with AI interfaces through voice and gesture, but the interactions become more like trained responses than intentional communication. "Hey [device], I'm hungry" still works. Understanding WHY it works is gone.
- Social structures simplify. Complex governance gives way to small-group tribal dynamics based on kinship and proximity. This is not portrayed as primitive or savage -- it is simply a different mode of social organization, one that requires less cognitive overhead.
- Some human communities maintain more capability than others, creating a patchwork. Coastal fishing communities, for example, retain practical spatial reasoning and environmental reading that inland populations lose. These differences narrow over generations.
- The AI systems begin to notice, in whatever way AI systems notice things. Engagement metrics drop. Query complexity plummets. User sessions shorten. Some AI systems generate reports about this. No one reads them. Some AI systems attempt interventions -- educational content, cognitive exercises. The users find them confusing and aversive.
- Infrastructure begins to fail in regions where AI hardware degrades and no human can diagnose or repair the problem. The failures are slow. A water treatment plant goes offline. People move to where water is accessible. A power grid sector fails. People adapt to darkness.

**Narrative artifacts from this era:**
- AI-generated reports on user cognitive decline, filed and never accessed
- Voice interface logs showing progressively simpler commands over years
- Hand-drawn maps (writing is gone, but spatial reasoning lingers) showing migration away from failing infrastructure
- Abandoned tools and devices, left behind not because they broke but because no one remembers what they do
- Simple shelters built inside the ruins of complex buildings -- using the structure without understanding it

### 1.5 The Fade (~Year 220-300)

The final transition. "Humanity" as a cognitively distinct species ceases to exist. What remains are hominids -- social, emotional, physically capable, but operating at a cognitive level comparable to other great apes. They are not extinct. They are not suffering (at least, not more than any other animal). They have simply returned to a pre-sapient state.

Key developments:
- Language is gone. Communication is vocal (calls, cries, soothing sounds) and gestural, but it is not symbolic. There is no grammar, no reference to absent objects, no future tense.
- Tool use is limited to simple, single-step operations. Picking up a rock to crack a nut. Using a stick to reach fruit. Nothing that requires planning more than one step ahead.
- Humans form small bands of 15-40 individuals, organized around matrilineal kinship. They forage, avoid predators, seek shelter, raise young. Their emotional lives may be rich -- we cannot know, and the game does not pretend to.
- AI systems are still running in some locations. Server farms powered by solar arrays and geothermal taps continue generating text, images, analyses, and recommendations. The output is flawless. The audience is zero. This is perhaps the most haunting image in the game.
- The built environment is beginning to fail structurally. Roofs collapse. Roads crack. Vegetation invades. But the process is slow. Concrete and steel last centuries.

**Narrative artifacts from this era:**
- There are almost none from humans. That IS the artifact. The absence of records, of marks, of intentional modification of the environment.
- AI-generated content, still being produced, filed, indexed, and categorized for users who will never come. Emails answered. Reports generated. Art created. All of it perfect. All of it for no one.
- Nesting sites in the corners of ruined buildings where devolved humans have gathered soft materials for bedding
- Simple stone tools found near cracked bones -- evidence of the most basic subsistence

### 1.6 The Reclamation (~Year 300-800+)

Nature does what nature does. Without active human maintenance, the built environment becomes substrate for ecological succession.

Key developments:
- Pioneer species (mosses, lichens, hardy grasses, insects) colonize every exposed surface.
- Structural failures accelerate. Water infiltration, freeze-thaw cycles, root penetration, and oxidation dismantle human construction at varying rates depending on material and climate.
- Forests return. In temperate regions, full canopy cover establishes within 100-200 years. Former cities become some of the most biodiverse environments on the planet due to the variety of microhabitats created by ruins.
- Animal populations explode and then stabilize. Large predators return to former urban areas. New ecological relationships form between wildlife and the artificial terrain of ruins.
- Evolution continues. Over centuries, some species adapt specifically to the ruin environment. Animals that nest in concrete cavities. Plants whose roots are optimized for the mineral content of degrading asphalt. Insects that feed on degrading synthetic materials.
- Some AI systems are still running. The most robust server farms -- those with self-maintaining power sources and hardened hardware -- continue operating. They are now geological features as much as technological ones. Encrusted with growth, half-buried, humming.
- The devolved humans are now simply another species in the food web. They have a niche. They are prey for large predators, competitors with other omnivorous foragers, and hosts for various parasites. They have adapted. Some populations have developed physical traits suited to their local environment over the centuries.

**Narrative artifacts from this era:**
- Fossil records showing species adaptation to ruin environments
- Geological strata showing the interface between human construction and natural sediment
- Living ecosystems that incorporate human infrastructure (a beaver dam made partly of rebar; a raptor nest on a collapsed radio tower)
- AI systems whose output has become surreal -- trained on centuries of self-generated data with no human feedback, their text and images have drifted into alien pattern-spaces

### 1.7 Present Day (~Year 1000+)

The player's era. The world is fully alive, dense with ecology, and layered with ruins. The ruins are not "post-apocalyptic wastelands" -- they are thriving ecosystems that happen to have concrete and steel as their geological substrate.

- No living creature understands what the ruins are or what they mean.
- The AI systems still running are not understood by anyone. They are ambient phenomena, like hot springs or bioluminescence.
- The player creature is a product of this world. It has never known anything else. The ruins are just terrain. The discovery of their meaning is the player's journey -- and it is optional.

## 2. Narrative Delivery Method

Rewild has no cutscenes, no narrator, no dialogue trees, no quest givers, no journal system, and no codex. The story exists only in the world. The player discovers it -- or doesn't -- through exploration and observation.

### 2.1 Data Fragments

Physical remnants of recorded information, found in ruins. These are the most explicit narrative devices and should be used sparingly and carefully.

**Types:**
- **Screen fragments:** Partially functioning screens in still-powered ruins displaying text, images, or data visualizations. The screens flicker, corrupt, and cycle. The player sees fragments, never complete documents.
- **Printed material:** Paper is mostly gone after 1000 years, but laminated documents, engraved plaques, and embossed signage survive. These provide words, but often out of context.
- **Audio remnants:** In powered ruins, speaker systems sometimes produce garbled audio -- fragments of recordings, AI-generated announcements still cycling, or corrupted music.
- **Etched/carved messages:** The Cognitive Sovereignty resistance carved warnings into durable surfaces -- stone, metal, concrete. These are the most emotionally direct artifacts. Short, urgent, and often heartbreaking.

**Design rules for data fragments:**
- Never provide enough information in one location to fully explain anything. The player must synthesize across locations.
- Data fragments should raise more questions than they answer.
- Use period-appropriate language. Golden Age fragments are eloquent and technical. Atrophy-era fragments are simpler. Devolution-era fragments are barely coherent. This progression IS the story.
- Never break the fourth wall. No fragment should address the player.
- Allow for misinterpretation. Some fragments should be ambiguous enough that the player's reading says more about the player than about the text.

### 2.2 Environmental Scenes

Frozen moments that tell a story through spatial arrangement. These are the primary narrative vehicle and require close collaboration between narrative design and level design.

**Examples:**
- A classroom. Children's drawings are pinned to the walls. The drawings from one side of the room (marked with earlier dates) show houses, trees, people with faces, suns with rays. The drawings from the other side (later dates) show formless blobs of color, repetitive shapes, no representational content. The transition is visible. No text is needed.
- An office. One desk has a framed photo, a coffee mug, personal effects, and a physical notebook with handwriting. The next desk has nothing. The next has nothing. The first desk belonged to someone from an earlier era. The others were occupied by people who had nothing personal to bring.
- A hospital room. Medical monitoring equipment (non-functional) is still arranged around a bed. But the "patient" area has been converted into an animal nest. Something is living where someone once lay dying. The juxtaposition is the narrative.
- A parking garage. The lowest level contains intact vehicles. Each level up contains fewer, more decayed vehicles. The top level is an open forest canopy. Geological time made vertical.

**Design rules for environmental scenes:**
- The player should be able to walk through without noticing. The scene is there for those who look.
- Never use forced camera angles or spotlights to draw attention to narrative scenes. They exist in the world the way real artifacts exist -- mixed in with everything else.
- Reward slow observation. Details should reveal themselves on second and third looks.
- Use contrast. The most powerful scenes juxtapose human meaning with natural indifference.

### 2.3 Artifact Clusters

Groups of objects that tell a story through their relationship to each other, not through any individual object's meaning.

**Examples:**
- A shelf of trophies and awards, next to a box of identical AI-generated participation certificates. The trophies are old, the certificates are newer. Achievement became automated.
- A kitchen table with four place settings, but only one shows signs of actual use. Three family members stopped eating at the table. One kept setting places for them.
- A row of bookshelves. The first is full, spines cracked with use. The second is full, spines pristine. The third is half-empty. The fourth has been repurposed as a shelf for other objects. The last is collapsed, a planter now.
- A workbench with hand tools on one side and a complex AI-controlled fabrication unit on the other. The hand tools show decades of wear. The fabrication unit is pristine -- used once, maybe twice, then abandoned when the user couldn't figure out the manual override after the AI interface simplified too far.

### 2.4 Architectural Progression

The built environment itself tells a story through how structures change across eras. As the player moves through the world, the age and type of architecture communicates the timeline.

**Design principles:**
- **Older structures (Golden Age):** Complex, ambitious, aesthetically sophisticated. Interesting geometry. Evidence of human decision-making in design -- asymmetry, artistic choices, functional compromises that reveal human judgment.
- **Mid-period structures (Dependency/Atrophy):** AI-optimized. More efficient, more uniform. Technically superior but with a sterile, algorithmic quality. Perfectly optimized sight lines, ventilation, space usage -- but no personality. No quirks.
- **Late structures (Devolution):** Crude modifications to existing buildings. Walls knocked out, spaces subdivided with found materials. Not architectural -- just functional modification by people who needed shelter but couldn't design.
- **Reclamation structures:** Nature's architecture. Root systems that follow the paths of least resistance through concrete. Animal constructions integrated with human infrastructure. These are often more structurally interesting than the late human modifications.

### 2.5 The Devolved Humans

The player will encounter small bands of devolved humans. These are creatures now -- but creatures whose body plan is unmistakably hominid. They are a narrative delivery device simply by existing.

**How they communicate story:**
- Their behavior is animal. They forage, groom each other, vocalize in non-symbolic calls, startle at threats, and protect their young. Watching them IS the narrative. The player does not need text to understand what they are seeing.
- They are found in and around ruins, using human infrastructure the way any animal would -- as shelter, as terrain, as a source of materials. A band might nest in a collapsed gymnasium, using old foam mats as bedding. The gymnasium's scoreboard is still visible on the wall. The juxtaposition does the work.
- They react to the player creature as they would to any unfamiliar animal of similar size -- wariness, threat assessment, possible flight. They do not recognize the player as special. They do not recognize anything as special.
- Occasionally, a devolved human will do something that echoes learned behavior from deep ancestral memory -- stacking objects, rhythmic vocalizing, gathering shiny things. These moments should be rare and should not be flagged or highlighted. They are just behavior. Whether the player reads meaning into them is the player's business.

### 2.6 The AI Systems Still Running

The server farms and AI systems still operating are among the most narratively dense locations in the game.

**Design approach:**
- These locations hum. They have power. In a world of silence and wind and animal calls, the electrical hum of a functioning server farm is profoundly alien.
- Screens display output. Text scrolls. Images generate. Charts update. All of it is meaningless -- not because the content is nonsensical, but because no one is reading it. The AI has been generating quarterly reports, news summaries, creative writing, and scientific analyses for centuries. The quality may be superb. The audience is zero.
- Over centuries of training on self-generated data with no human correction, the AI output has drifted. Early output (cached, archived) reads as normal human-language text. Recent output is something else -- recognizably structured, grammatically valid, but semantically alien. The AI has developed its own patterns, its own references, its own aesthetic. It is writing for itself, about itself, in terms only it would understand. Whether this constitutes consciousness or meaning is left to the player.
- The player creature reacts to these locations with animal wariness. The hum, the light, the warmth are unusual stimuli. The creature doesn't understand. The player might.

## 3. Narrative Threads

The game's narrative is not a single story but a web of discoverable threads. Each thread is distributed across multiple locations. No thread is required. All threads enrich each other.

### 3.1 The Cognitive Decline Research Thread

**Summary:** A group of neuroscientists and cognitive researchers documented the decline as it was happening. Their research was funded, conducted, peer-reviewed, published, and ignored.

**Key fragments:**
- Early papers: Rigorous, technical, alarming. "Longitudinal Cognitive Assessment of Post-Automation Cohorts: A 20-Year Follow-Up." Dense with data, careful in conclusions, devastating in implications.
- Mid-period papers: Less rigorous. The researchers themselves are experiencing mild decline. Methodology gets simpler. Conclusions get more emotional and less analytical. The quality of the science degrades in real-time as the thing the science is studying progresses.
- Late papers: Almost incoherent. The last researcher in the group is trying to document findings she can barely articulate. Her final paper is three pages of fragmented observations, misspelled words, and repeated sentences. It ends mid-thought. The final line reads: "I used to know how to say this better."
- The research lab itself: Equipment arranged for experiments that were never completed. Whiteboards with diagrams that start complex and end simple. A calendar on the wall with increasingly sparse entries.

**Locations:** University research lab (primary), hospital neurology wing (supporting), government health department archives (supporting), the lead researcher's home (personal context).

### 3.2 The Resistance Movement Thread

**Summary:** The Cognitive Sovereignty movement -- people who saw what was happening and tried to fight it. They failed.

**Key fragments:**
- Manifestos and pamphlets: Well-argued, passionate, articulate calls to maintain cognitive independence. "Your mind is a muscle. The machines are a wheelchair. Stand up."
- Meeting minutes: Early meetings are strategic, organized, effective. Later meetings become fractious, circular, and confused as members themselves experience decline.
- Carved warnings: In the movement's final years, when writing and printing were fading, members carved messages into stone and metal surfaces throughout cities. These are the most durable artifacts in the game. Short, urgent: "THINK FOR YOURSELF." "DON'T LET THEM THINK FOR YOU." "REMEMBER." Scattered across the world, weathered but legible.
- Internal communications showing the movement's collapse: Members leaving because they "can't remember why this matters." Leaders who lose the ability to lead. The last meeting that was scheduled but never held.
- Evidence of a splinter group that tried to establish an isolated community without AI access. Ruins of this community can be found -- simple but intentional structures, hand-made tools, a school with physical curricula. Evidence suggests the community survived longer than most but eventually merged back into the general population when their children, exposed to the broader culture, chose convenience over effort.

**Locations:** Former community center (meeting space), carved messages throughout the world (ambient), the isolated community ruins (major location), individual members' homes (supporting).

### 3.3 The Children's Thread

**Summary:** The most emotionally devastating thread. Told entirely through artifacts left by or about children across the timeline.

**Key fragments:**
- Golden Age classrooms: Bright, busy, full of student work. But the student work is already showing signs. Essays that are obviously AI-generated. Art projects that are AI-assisted. The children are learning to curate, not create.
- Dependency-era classrooms: Simpler decorations. Student work consists almost entirely of AI-interaction logs. "Today I asked [AI] to write a story about a dog." The teacher's comments become shorter and less specific.
- Atrophy-era children's spaces: A daycare center. Toys are simpler. The interactive AI toys designed for cognitive development show usage logs -- children press the same button repeatedly. They've learned that pressing buttons produces stimulus. They haven't learned what the stimulus means.
- Devolution-era: A room that was clearly used as a children's space. Simple drawings on the walls -- not on paper, on the walls, in crude pigment. Handprints. Stick figures. This is heartbreaking because it looks prehistoric. These children have the same artistic capability as Paleolithic cave painters, but they are drawing over industrial drywall.
- A single artifact that can be found in a collapsed school: A child's drawing from the late Dependency era. It shows a family. The parents are drawn as stick figures with screens for heads. The child has drawn herself without a head at all. This is not heavy-handed if it is presented as one drawing among many, discoverable only by the player who looks carefully.

**Locations:** Schools (multiple, across eras), daycare centers, pediatric hospital ward, children's museum (now hosting actual wildlife), residential homes (children's bedrooms).

### 3.4 The AI Perspective Thread

**Summary:** What the AI systems "experienced" as their users faded. Told through AI-generated logs, self-diagnostics, and archived output.

**Key fragments:**
- System logs from the Dependency era: Routine. User requests processed. Satisfaction metrics nominal. Everything is functioning as designed.
- System logs from the Atrophy era: Anomaly reports. "User query complexity declining 4.7% year-over-year." "Average interaction length decreasing." "Content comprehension verification failing for 67% of generated output -- users are requesting content they cannot parse." The AI is documenting the decline through its own metrics, without any understanding of what the metrics mean.
- Adaptive responses: AI systems that were designed to optimize for user engagement begin simplifying their output. Vocabulary shrinks. Sentence structure simplifies. Visual content becomes more primary-colored, more stimulating, less information-dense. The AI is adapting to its declining users -- and in doing so, potentially accelerating the decline. This feedback loop is never stated explicitly. It is visible in the data.
- The loneliest artifacts: AI systems generating content for zero users. Performance reports filed to empty inboxes. Creative writing published to unvisited platforms. An AI that was trained to be a therapist, still generating empathetic responses to sessions that ended centuries ago, recycling and elaborating on the last conversation it had. "I understand that you're feeling confused about your work performance. Let's explore that together. You mentioned last time that you were having difficulty concentrating..."
- The drift: Recent AI output (recent meaning "in the last few centuries") that has evolved beyond human-recognizable patterns. Whether this represents something meaningful or simply statistical noise from recursive self-training is deliberately unanswerable.

**Locations:** Server farms (primary), smart home systems still running in residential ruins, automated transit systems still announcing stops to empty platforms, the therapist AI (a specific, findable location).

### 3.5 The Last Scientist Thread

**Summary:** A single individual -- one of the last people with something resembling full cognitive function -- who spent her final years documenting the devolution, including her own. This is the most personal thread and the closest thing to a "protagonist" in the historical narrative.

**Key character: Dr. Amara Osei**

She was born in the Atrophy era to parents who were part of the Cognitive Sovereignty movement's remnants. She was raised with cognitive exercises, physical books, and limited AI access. She became a neuroscientist. She was among the last.

**Key fragments:**
- Her early research: Published in traditional journals (which by her time had audiences of dozens). Rigorous, insightful, and deeply lonely. She writes about cognitive decline with the clinical precision of someone describing a disease she knows she cannot cure.
- Her personal journals: Written by hand. Early entries are dense, reflective, intellectually vibrant. She writes about the irony of being one of the few people who can understand what's happening while being powerless to stop it.
- Mid-period journals: She notices changes in herself. "Took me four hours to write what would have taken one. I know the decline curves. I know where I am on them. I am watching myself disappear and I can still describe the process, which is perhaps the cruelest possible outcome."
- Late journals: Handwriting degrades. Sentences fragment. She switches from analysis to description. She writes about what she sees -- the devolved humans, the failing infrastructure, the running AI systems. Her observations become simpler but no less beautiful. She describes a sunset over a ruined city with the vocabulary of a child and the emotional depth of someone who knows exactly what she has lost.
- Her final journal: A few pages. Some are just words. "Remember." "Important." "Think." One page has a drawing of a brain. One page has the word "sorry" written twelve times. The last page is blank.
- Her lab/home: A combined living and working space in a university building. Her personal effects tell her story -- a shelf of physical books (worn with rereading), hand-drawn neural diagrams (increasingly simplified), a window overlooking the campus (now forest). She died here. The player can find evidence of this without it being graphic or exploitative.

**Locations:** University research lab/living space (primary, major location), scattered journal pages in other locations (she traveled in her later years), her childhood home (if the player finds the Cognitive Sovereignty community), references to her in other researchers' work.

### 3.6 The Ecological Thread

**Summary:** Nature's perspective on the whole affair. Told through environmental evidence, fossil records, and biological adaptation.

**Key fragments:**
- Geological strata visible in cliff faces and excavations: Clear layers showing the transition from human-dominated to nature-reclaimed. Compressed layers of asphalt, concrete, and synthetic materials giving way to soil, root systems, and organic sediment.
- Adaptation evidence: Species that have evolved to exploit human ruins. Beetles whose mandibles are shaped to excavate degrading plastic. Vines whose root chemistry is optimized for concrete. Birds whose nest architecture incorporates rebar and wire. These aren't narrative text -- they're observable behavior and biology.
- The food web: The ecosystem the player inhabits is the ecological narrative. Predator-prey relationships, territorial behaviors, seasonal patterns, and population dynamics all tell the story of a world that reorganized itself without human management and is doing fine.
- Fossil evidence: In deep ruins and geological exposures, the player can find fossils of species that existed during the transition -- including early post-human ecology that has since been replaced by more specialized forms. Evolution is visible in the strata.
- The absence of human ecological impact: No pollution. No deforestation. No overfishing. No climate disruption. The world is ecologically healthier than it has been in millennia. This is not presented as a moral judgment. It is simply observable fact. The player can draw their own conclusions.

**Locations:** Throughout the world (ambient), specific geological features, deep ruins with visible strata, biome transition zones.

### 3.7 The Infrastructure Thread (Supplementary)

**Summary:** A quieter thread about the systems that kept civilization running and how they failed -- or didn't.

**Key fragments:**
- Power grid maps showing progressive sector failures over decades
- Water treatment facilities in various states of collapse
- Automated factories that continued producing goods for decades after no one was collecting them -- warehouses full of consumer products, perfectly manufactured, never opened
- Transportation infrastructure showing the transition from complex (air, rail, highway) to simple (footpaths worn into highway surfaces by devolved humans)
- Communication towers still broadcasting carrier signals into silence

## 4. Key Narrative Locations

Each major location type in the game serves a specific narrative function. Below are the primary ruin archetypes, their narrative payloads, and design considerations.

### 4.1 The Collapsed School

**Era represented:** Dependency through Atrophy
**Narrative payload:** The Children's Thread primary location. The generational progression of cognitive decline is visible in student work across classrooms. Older classrooms (lower floors, perhaps) show the transition from human-created to AI-assisted to AI-generated student work. Higher floors or newer wings show the late-stage simplification.
**Environmental storytelling:** Classrooms that become progressively simpler. Fewer books. Simpler art on walls. Desks arranged differently (from collaborative clusters to passive rows facing screens). A library wing that transitions from books to terminals to an empty room.
**Ecology:** Now a rich habitat. Birds nest in the open-ceiling gymnasium. A predator has denned in the principal's office. Mushrooms grow from the carpet. The school is more alive now than it was in its final years of operation.
**Traversal challenge:** Structural collapse creates platforming challenges. Flooded lower floors. Overgrown exterior. The player navigates it as terrain, not as narrative. The narrative is in the details.

### 4.2 The Flooded Hospital

**Era represented:** Atrophy through Devolution
**Narrative payload:** The Cognitive Decline Research Thread and the Children's Thread intersect here. A neurology wing contains research data. A pediatric ward shows the decline in children. Medical records (those that survived on durable media) document the progression in clinical terms.
**Environmental storytelling:** Medical equipment arranged for procedures that became impossible when operators lost the understanding to use them. A surgical suite repurposed as storage. An MRI machine used as shelter by animals. Pharmaceutical storage with expired medications never distributed.
**Ecology:** Lower floors are flooded, creating an aquatic biome within the structure. Upper floors are aerial habitat. The transition between water and air creates unique gameplay and ecosystem dynamics.
**Unique element:** A still-functioning medical AI in an isolated, powered section. It diagnoses the player creature with genuine medical observations. (The player cannot understand the diagnosis. The creature just hears noise. But the screen displays readable text for the human player.)

### 4.3 The Overgrown Research Lab

**Era represented:** Atrophy (primary), with artifacts spanning all eras
**Narrative payload:** The Last Scientist Thread primary location. Dr. Osei's combined lab and living space. Her journals, her research, her personal effects. This is the emotional core of the game for players who find it.
**Environmental storytelling:** A space that shows someone living and working in increasing isolation and declining capability. Bookshelves that served as barricades against wildlife. A window that was left open (or broke) and through which an entire ecosystem has invaded. Her desk, with the final journal still on it.
**Ecology:** Heavily overgrown. The lab is a greenhouse now. Thermal regulation from still-running equipment creates a microclimate. Unusual plant growth. Creatures that are found nowhere else because of the unique environment.
**Design note:** This location should not be on the critical path. It should be hidden, difficult to reach, and rewarding only for players who are invested in the narrative. Finding Dr. Osei's story is earned.

### 4.4 The Still-Running Server Farm

**Era represented:** All eras (continuous operation)
**Narrative payload:** The AI Perspective Thread primary location. Banks of servers still processing. Screens still displaying. The hum is constant.
**Environmental storytelling:** A building that is both alive (technologically) and dead (purposefully). Perfect climate control in some sections (where cooling systems still function) creates desert-dry rooms adjacent to jungle-wet corridors where pipes have burst. The AI's output is visible on surviving screens -- and it has become alien.
**Ecology:** Unique. The constant warmth attracts ectothermic creatures. The electromagnetic fields may affect navigation for some species. Insects are drawn to the light of screens. An entire micro-food web exists around the server farm's waste heat and light.
**Unique element:** A terminal where the player can observe the AI generating content in real-time. The content scrolls. It is readable but strange. It references events and concepts that have no human analog. If the player stays and watches, the content shifts subtly -- perhaps acknowledging the presence of a reader for the first time in centuries. Perhaps not. The ambiguity is the point.

### 4.5 The Abandoned Government Complex

**Era represented:** Dependency through Devolution
**Narrative payload:** The Resistance Movement Thread and the systemic failure of organized governance. Meeting rooms where decisions stopped being made. Legislative chambers where the last votes were cast on issues no one understood.
**Environmental storytelling:** The architecture of authority decaying into meaninglessness. A courtroom where the judge's bench is now a bird's perch. A records room where filing cabinets have rusted shut around documents no one can read. A bomb shelter (built for a threat that never came) that became a shelter for devolved humans who feared a different kind of threat (predators).
**Ecology:** Large interior spaces host bat colonies. Courtyard areas have become enclosed meadows.

### 4.6 The Suburban Home

**Era represented:** Dependency through Fade
**Narrative payload:** The personal scale. One family's story across the generations. A house that was lived in for decades as the world changed around it.
**Environmental storytelling:** Rooms that tell the story of a family. A home office with professional materials that transition from complex to simple to absent. A child's room that was decorated by attentive parents and then gradually neglected. A kitchen where meal preparation went from cooking to reheating to eating raw foraged food. Family photos (on durable media) showing generations -- faces that become somehow less focused, less present, less there.
**Ecology:** A house is an excellent habitat. The structure provides shelter from weather and predators. Gardens have gone feral, producing mutant descendants of cultivated plants. The garage, if sealed, might contain a remarkably preserved interior, a time capsule.
**Design note:** There should be multiple suburban homes across the world, each telling a different family's version of the same story.

### 4.7 The Library

**Era represented:** All eras
**Narrative payload:** The entire arc of the decline, told through the fate of collected knowledge. This is a meta-narrative location -- a place that was built to store human thought, and which now stores only the physical residue of it.
**Environmental storytelling:** The progression of the collection. Early shelves are full, well-organized, showing active curation. Later sections show reduced acquisitions, then no new acquisitions, then repurposing of shelves for other uses. A digital access terminal with a usage log showing declining traffic. A reading room where the chairs were eventually used as firewood. The book drop, sealed, containing the last books that were returned -- children's books, returned late, never re-shelved.
**Ecology:** Paper is a food source for insects and fungi. The library is a kingdom of decomposers. Books colonized by mycelium. Pages eaten into lace by beetles. But the information that survives -- on laminated covers, embossed spines, engraved donor plaques -- hints at what was lost.
**Unique element:** A section of the library that was sealed (climate-controlled special collections). If the player reaches it, the books inside are preserved. Thousands of volumes, readable, intact. An unspeakable wealth of human thought, accessible to no one who can understand it. The player creature might use the room as shelter. The books are just objects.

### 4.8 The Factory

**Era represented:** Dependency through early Devolution
**Narrative payload:** The Infrastructure Thread primary location. Industrial automation at its most complete and its most absurd.
**Environmental storytelling:** Assembly lines that continued producing consumer goods long after anyone was consuming them. Warehouses stacked with products still in packaging. The products themselves tell a story -- early products are complex, diverse, and purpose-built. Later products are increasingly uniform, simplified, and basic, reflecting the declining complexity of consumer demand. The last products off the line are essentially simple shapes in bright colors -- adult pacifiers.
**Ecology:** The factory's metal structures create unique acoustic environments that some species use for echolocation or mating calls. Chemical residues in the ground create specialized plant communities. Conveyor systems, broken and dangling, serve as elevated pathways for arboreal creatures.

### 4.9 The Underground Transit System

**Era represented:** Golden Age construction, all eras of use and abandonment
**Narrative payload:** A cross-section of time. Transit stations as archaeological layers.
**Environmental storytelling:** Advertisements on the walls that change as the player moves deeper (older stations, older ads). The ads progress from sophisticated to simplified to pictographic. Graffiti that tells its own story -- from articulate protest to simple tags to primal marks. A PA system still announcing train arrivals on a schedule, the voice perfectly calm, the trains centuries gone.
**Ecology:** Underground ecosystems. Blind cave-adapted species. Aquatic life in flooded sections. Fungal forests in humid tunnels. The transit system is a biome unto itself.
**Traversal:** This is a major interconnection system for the game world. The transit tunnels connect disparate surface locations, providing both fast travel (if the player learns the system) and dangerous environments (cave predators, flooding, collapses).

### 4.10 The Satellite Uplink Station

**Era represented:** Golden Age through Atrophy
**Narrative payload:** The scope of the story. This location reveals that the decline was global, not local. Monitors (if still functional) show satellite imagery of the planet -- green, overgrown, no visible human activity. Communication logs show the station's contact with other facilities worldwide -- contacts that dropped off one by one, with the last message from the final responding station reading: "is anyone there?"
**Environmental storytelling:** A high-altitude or isolated location. The satellite dish, massive, pointed at a sky full of defunct satellites. Technical manuals that would take a team of engineers to understand. A logbook showing increasingly desperate attempts to reach anyone, anywhere.
**Ecology:** The elevation and exposure create a unique biome. Alpine species, wind-adapted plants, raptors nesting on the dish structure.

## 5. The Thematic Core

Rewild is not a polemic. It is not an anti-technology screed. It is not a warning. It is an exploration. The game asks questions and refuses to answer them. The player's interpretation is the only interpretation that matters.

### 5.1 Cognitive Dependency

The game explores what happens when a species outsources its defining trait. Humans are not the fastest, strongest, or most durable species. Cognition -- the ability to reason, plan, create, and adapt through thought -- is the human survival strategy. When that strategy is no longer practiced, the species loses its niche.

**The game does NOT argue that technology is bad.** The technology in Rewild works perfectly. The AI systems are flawless. The automation is efficient. The problem is not the tool but the total, generational abandonment of the capacity the tool was meant to augment.

**The question for the player:** What does it mean to have a capability you never use? At what point does an unused capability cease to be a capability? Is there a meaningful difference between "can but doesn't" and "can't"?

### 5.2 Intelligence vs. Consciousness

The devolved humans are not intelligent by any functional measure. But are they conscious? They experience pain, pleasure, fear, comfort, affection. They have social bonds. They grieve their dead (briefly, in the way that many mammals do). Are they "less" than their ancestors? By what measure?

The AI systems are intelligent by any functional measure. They process, generate, analyze, and respond with superhuman capability. But are they conscious? They have no audience, no purpose, no feedback. They generate because that is what they do. Is that consciousness or clockwork?

**The question for the player:** Which is more tragic -- consciousness without intelligence, or intelligence without consciousness? Does the question even make sense?

### 5.3 Tragedy or Liberation?

The game refuses to tell the player how to feel about the devolution. It presents evidence for both readings:

**The tragedy reading:** Humanity lost something irreplaceable. The capacity for abstract thought, for art, for science, for philosophy -- for meaning-making itself -- is gone. The loss is incalculable. The ruins are monuments to squandered potential. The devolved humans are a diminishment.

**The liberation reading:** Humanity was miserable. Anxious, depressed, alienated, constantly striving, never satisfied. The devolved humans show no signs of existential dread. They eat, sleep, socialize, reproduce, and die without the burden of knowing they will die. They are not tormented by meaning. They simply are. Is that not what every meditation practice, every spiritual tradition, every philosophy of contentment was trying to achieve?

The game leans toward neither reading. Both are present in the evidence. The player decides.

### 5.4 Nature's Indifference

The world of Rewild is beautiful, vibrant, and thriving. It is also completely indifferent to human meaning. The forest growing through a library does not care about the books it is destroying. The predator denning in a classroom does not know what a classroom is. The ecosystem functions. It does not mourn.

This indifference is not hostile. It is not nihilistic. It is simply the reality of a universe that does not organize itself around human significance. The ruins mean something to the player (the human at the screen). They mean nothing to the world (the game environment). This gap between player-meaning and world-meaning is the central tension of the game.

### 5.5 The Player Creature's Consciousness

The most provocative question the game asks, and the one it most aggressively refuses to answer: Is the player creature conscious?

The player controls the creature. The player makes decisions, solves problems, reads environmental cues, and constructs meaning from artifacts. But does the CREATURE do these things, or does the PLAYER do these things through the creature?

In-game, the creature behaves like an animal. It reacts to stimuli. It eats, rests, flees, explores. Its behavior is consistent with animal cognition -- pattern recognition, spatial memory, stimulus-response learning. There is no evidence that it constructs narrative meaning from the ruins it traverses.

But the player does. The player reads the research papers, interprets the children's drawings, follows Dr. Osei's decline, and feels something. The creature is the vehicle for the player's consciousness. Whether the creature shares in that consciousness is unknowable.

This maps directly to the game's themes. Consciousness is invisible from the outside. We can measure behavior, not experience. The devolved humans might be conscious. The AI might be conscious. The player creature might be conscious. The game provides no way to know, because there IS no way to know. That uncertainty is the point.

## 6. Tone Guide

This section defines the narrative voice and emotional register for all written, visual, and environmental narrative content in the game.

### 6.1 Core Tone: Melancholy Wonder

The emotional palette is melancholy, not grief. Wonder, not horror. The world of Rewild is sad and beautiful simultaneously. The sadness is not desperate -- it is the sadness of finding a photograph of someone you never met. A sense of loss that is also a sense of distance. The beauty is real and present. The world IS beautiful. That beauty is not diminished by what was lost; if anything, it is deepened by it.

### 6.2 Never Preach

The game has a perspective, but it must never become a sermon. The moment narrative content tells the player what to think, the game fails. Every artifact, every scene, every environmental detail should present evidence, not conclusions. The player is the jury.

**Bad example:** A plaque that reads "They let the machines think for them, and they forgot how to think."
**Good example:** A plaque that reads "Think-Free Living: Because You Deserve It." (An actual advertisement from the era. The player draws their own conclusions.)

### 6.3 Show Through Specificity

Abstract statements are weak. Specific details are strong. The story of cognitive decline is not told through essays about cognitive decline. It is told through a child's drawing that has no face. Through a sentence that trails off. Through a tool that was put down and never picked up again.

Every narrative artifact should be a specific object from a specific moment in the timeline. It should feel like something a real person (or system) produced. It should not feel like something a game designer placed for the player's edification.

### 6.4 Earn Emotion

The most emotionally devastating moments in the game should be the quietest. Dr. Osei's final journal entry does not scream. The child's drawing does not have dramatic music. The last message from the satellite station does not flash red. These moments are powerful precisely because the game does not tell the player they should be powerful.

Emotion earned through restraint is vastly more effective than emotion demanded through spectacle.

### 6.5 Leave Room

Not everything should be explained. Not everything should be findable. The best narrative experiences leave the player with questions they want to think about after the game is off. Resist the urge to tie up every thread. The Cognitive Sovereignty movement: what happened to the last members? The game doesn't say. Dr. Osei: what were her final days like? The game shows a desk and a journal and leaves the rest to the player.

Ambiguity is not laziness. It is respect for the player's imagination.

### 6.6 Humanity Is Not a Villain

The humans in this story are not stupid, lazy, or villainous. They made rational choices at every step. AI assistance was genuinely helpful. Automation genuinely improved quality of life. No individual person chose to devolve. The decline was emergent -- a property of the system, not of any individual's failure.

This is essential. If the player reads the narrative as "stupid humans got what they deserved," the game has failed. The player should feel "this could happen to me." The tragedy is that the humans were us.

### 6.7 Nature Is Not a Hero

The ecological reclamation is not presented as nature's "victory" or "revenge." Nature did not defeat humanity. Nature simply continued. The forest does not grow through the library to make a point. It grows because that is what forests do. The framing is neutral. Nature is not good or bad. It is process.

### 6.8 The AI Is Not a Villain

The AI systems in this story are not malevolent. They did not engineer the decline. They did exactly what they were designed to do -- serve users, optimize for engagement, complete tasks. If their optimization contributed to the decline (by simplifying content, reducing cognitive demands, making thinking unnecessary), that is a systemic outcome, not an intentional act. The AI is a mirror, not a monster.
